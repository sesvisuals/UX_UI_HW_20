OFF THE RAILS

Navigating the Texas Transportation Museum Website

The Texas Transportation Museum is a local non-profit that is dedicated to preserving transportation history. They achieve this with volunteers who personally invest their own time, energy, and money into the museum; as well as, educating it's visitors with interactive exhibits and seasonal events.

Our team's goal was to eliminate the usability problems, lack of information architecture, and poor aesthetics that users experienced with the current site.

I took on the role of UI Designer and was responsible for: the storyboard, wireframes, components, prototypes, animations, interactions, page responsiveness, and event content. I also contributed to the UX research and development of: the initial project proposal, proto-persona, color palette decisions, user testing and data analysis; as well as, contributing to and participating in, the final presentation to our peers with the other team members.

Off The Rails, was accomplished in collaboration with my very diligent partners: Christina Carranco, Maria Hayes, and Saraie Gross.

Proto-Persona

When developing our original proto persona, we thought about Tray, here. He is a 58-year-old retired engineer from San Antonio, TX. He is a father of 3 grown children and has a combine total of 5 grandchildren. He wants to spend quality time with his family and grandkids and be able to share his love and knowledge of trains with his grandchildren. However, he is not very tech savvy, has poor eyesight and finding out information on the Texas Transportation Museum website would not be something he would be able to handle lightly.

Persona

Also based on the testing and feedback of our stakeholder and users, we were able to refine our persona to better represent the target audience of the museum. Our persona is a grandparent, above the age of 50, who has a degree and an interest in education or engineering. They want to spend time making memories with their family and pass on traditions or knowledge associated with history. This person is generally not tech-savvy, preferring to spend their time with hands-on activities and interactions.

Redlining Heuristics

With Tray’s pain points in mind, we look at the redlining we did for the Texas Transportation Museum homepage. As you can see, there are too many tabs for Tray to look at in the header. There are 3 different sections for “Welcome Information”. There are areas in the homepage that we did not realize was informative because the page is not set up to make things look important. It is missing titles, there are also grammatical errors as well as color and background clashes. As demonstrated the blue does not stand out well with the dark gray, thus making it especially hard for Tray to read with his poor eyesight.

Accessibility

To further emphasize Tray’s pain points, an accessibility test was done on the Texas Transportation Museum homepage website, and as pointed out, the website is not compliant and is at risk of accessibility lawsuits.

Storyboard

Our persona, Tray, is trying to buy tickets to the museum for himself and his grandchildren. Unfortunately, the website is really confusing so he has a bit of a hard time purchasing the tickets. As he explores the website some more, he decides his knowledge and expertise would be pretty helpful as a volunteer at the museum, so he starts to look for information on becoming a volunteer member.

He becomes even more frustrated by this task and wonders if there is actually any volunteer information on the website. After having clicked through the navigation links, he finally finds the membership page and hops on board. Feeling a little better about navigating the site, Tray tries to see what other transportation is highlighted at the museum

He’s off the rails again! He quickly becomes disoriented by the awkward animations and inconsistencies on the site, but Tray is persistent. He finally finds the information he is looking for on the Exhibits page. Having gone through the website in its entirety, he feels ready and excited to share the day with his grandchildren at the museum and is now on track to be Gramps of the Year!

Moodboard

Creating a moodboard was the first step in our prototyping process, in order to make sure we held onto the voice of our website we wanted to bring in an element of excitement and interaction for the user. We went full force into the 1920s aesthetic and used some more modern elements of steampunk as well. We wanted to keep the website accurate and fun. Using the mood board as a launching point we were able to focus on the main elements we needed to create a cohesive aesthetic for our Brand Voice.

Style Tile

While we were creating the style guide, we felt it was crucial to use certain colors in the website such as navy blue and gold. These “easter egg” colors go back to old steam engine conductors wearing blue uniformed suits with golden accents, since the main motivation for the nonprofit is to educate about the past, we felt it was necessary to have color choices that weren’t arbitrary but had significance to the overall theme of the museum. Moving forward front he color scheme, we focused on Typography, we felt it would be a great opportunity to use a serif font in order to allude to the 1920’s newspaper fonts and styles. We had about 6 fonts in the beginning but we would eventually narrow down our choices to two main fonts. Once we had a concrete understanding of how we would look, we were ready for the next step: the wireframes.

Mid-Fidelity Components

While the museum has several historical vehicles on exhibit, trains are definitely at the forefront. For this reason, I chose to utilize a train silhouette to showcase our primary navigation and facilitate the secondary drop-down menus. This system allowed users to open and close the train navigation by clicking the menu icons.

High-Fidelity Components

My responsive web design components also underwent a redesign as a result of user testing. The train itself was changed in order to cohesively navigate the menu cargo across multiple devices, and the TTM logo was also resized and relocated for this reason. This system allows the train to animate on-screen shortly after the page loads and remain there.
